Session Playbook

How to play this

50 minutes. This is not a whiteboard — it's a technical deep dive and trust-building exercise. Pamela is showing us her work. Our job is to prove we can engage at her level and be worth introducing to Vijay and Onyeka.

Acknowledge that this session is different. We're not here to present — we're here to understand. "Pamela, thank you for carving out the time. We know you're building something important with the reference architecture and the AI operating model — we're genuinely here to learn how you're thinking about it. Walk us through it."

Then stop talking. Let her drive. The first 5 minutes sets the dynamic for the whole session — if we start talking about ourselves, we've lost.

This is the core of the session. Pamela will walk through her reference architecture and AI operating model. Take notes. Ask follow-up questions that show you're tracking the detail, not just nodding:

  • "When you say reference architecture — is this a technology stack view, a governance framework, or both?" Shows you know these are different things.
  • "How does a use case move from identification to production in the operating model? Where are the gates?" Shows you understand delivery pipelines in regulated environments.
  • "You mentioned your own AI model — what layer of the architecture does it sit in, and what's above and below it?" Technical depth without being showy.
  • "Where does the Trust & Safety council intersect with the delivery pipeline? Is it a gate or an advisory function?" Shows you understand healthcare governance isn't optional.
  • "What's the biggest friction point right now — is it identifying the right use cases, or getting them from approved to live?" Surfaces the real bottleneck.

The goal: By the end of this block, you should be able to draw their architecture back to them on a whiteboard. If you can't, you haven't listened hard enough.

Once Pamela has shared her framework, this is where you earn the trust. Not by pitching — by demonstrating you can think at the same level. Share relevant parallels only where they genuinely connect:

  • If she describes a delivery bottleneck: "We see this pattern a lot — the architecture is sound but the execution bandwidth isn't there. The way we've worked with other organisations in regulated industries is to own specific use cases end-to-end within their existing framework, at their risk."
  • If she describes limits of their AI model: "That's an interesting boundary. What we've seen is that predictive models are strong for classification and scoring, but the multi-step workflow orchestration — the agentic layer — is where a lot of organisations are hitting the ceiling. That's our territory."
  • If she asks about healthcare experience directly: Be honest. "We've done deep work in regulated industries — clinical trials, insurance, major banks — where data sensitivity and compliance governance are non-negotiable. Healthcare is new ground for us, but the muscle memory around regulated delivery is real. We'd rather be honest about that than oversell."

The rule: Only share a Conquer perspective if it genuinely advances the conversation. If you're not sure, don't. Saying less and listening more is always the safer bet in a trust-building session.

Reflect back what you heard. Be specific — not "that was great" but a concrete summary: "Pamela, here's what I'm taking away: you're building a reference architecture around [X principles], the operating model runs use cases through [Y pipeline], your AI model handles [Z] but you're looking at [gap]. Does that track?"

Let her correct you. The corrections are gold — they show her you're engaged and they give you more detail.

Then the close: "We'd love to go away, digest this properly, and come back with a short perspective on where we think Conquer could genuinely add value within your framework — not a pitch, just a view on how what we do might complement what you're building. Would it be useful if we shared that with you? And would it make sense to involve Vijay and Onyeka in that conversation?"

Don't propose scope. Don't name a price. Don't commit to a timeline. Just get the mandate to come back with something thoughtful. If Pamela says "let me set up a call with Vijay and Onyeka" — you've won.
